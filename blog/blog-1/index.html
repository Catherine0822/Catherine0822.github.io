Can we improve the aging group’s acceptance of social robots by observational learning?

In this research, we collaborate with ASUS, the leading electronics company in Taiwan, to use their social robot, Zenbo, to explore the effect of observational learning on older people’s acceptance toward the social robot.
Results show that observational learning can be helpful for improving the aging groups positive attitudes toward robots, although direct interaction is still required as a moderator.

What is Observational Learning?

A way of learning that consists of observing and modeling another individual’s behavior, attitudes, or emotional expressions.

“Learning would be exceedingly laborious, not to mention hazardous, if people had to rely solely on the effects of their own actions to inform them what to do. Fortunately, most human behavior is learned observationally through modeling: from observing others one forms an idea of how new behaviors are performed, and on later occasions this coded information serves as a guide for action.” -- Bandura (1997)

Problem

  • Prior research find that older adults had higher negative attitudes toward robots, compared with younger adults, but their acceptance toward robots can be enhanced after direct interaction with robots (Chien et al, 2019).
  • However, older adults may resist interacting with robots in the first place due to their negative attitude toward robots, which prevents them from getting along with robots further.
  • We want to seek possible alternative options, other than direct interaction, to enhance older adults’ acceptance toward robots.
  • Some prior research found that observational learning can be used in learning social interaction (Bandura, 1961) and new technologies (Yi, M. Y., & Davis, F. D., 2003).

Research Question

Can we improve the aging group's acceptance of robots with observational learning?

Methods

  • Participants
    • We recruited 80 participants, 40 younger adults and 40 older adults
    • 4 groups in total. Each group has 20 people (2 x 2 between subject design)
      • Young adults x older adults
      • control x treatment (observational learning) group
  • Intervention

    Each Participant watched a 3 minutes long video

    • The treatment groups watch a video in which someone is interacting with the robot Zenbo
      • interaction video (Click to watch)
    • The control groups, in contrast, watch a video in which the robot Zenbo is introducing itself
      • no interaction video (Click to watch)
  • Procedures
  • Measures
    Several surveys and computerized cognitive testing are used for measuring implicit, explicit attitudes, and personality.
    • Surveys
      • Trait curiosity questionnaire
      • Big Five Personality Test
      • Unified Theory of Acceptance and Use of Technology (UTAUT)
      • State curiosity questionnaire
      • Subjective Technology Adaptivity Inventory (STAI)
    • Computerized cognitive testing
      • Implicit association task (IAT)
      • perceptual matching task

Main Findings

  1. The effect of observational learning appeared after direct interaction with Zenbo. The observational groups show higher scores in perceived safety of technology (PST) in STAI.

  2. In general, participants with higher UTAUT score, which indicates a higher self-report acceptence, were more willing to stay and to interact with the robot.

  3. In the observational learning group, participants with a higher state curiosity also had a higher use intention toward the robot.

  4. Regardless of age and video type, the D1 score of IAT declined after watching the video. The result implied that the implicit attitude toward robots could be changed in a short period of time (A high D1 score means a higher negative attitude).

Reflections

  1. The necessity of direct interaction
    Our results shows that the effect of observational learning appeared after direct interaction with Zenbo, inferring direct interaction still has its necessity. Despite that, we still find that observational learning has some impact on the acceptance of robots.

  2. Too many measures
    Too many measures in one research might lead to participant fatigue. For example, for meassuring implicit attitudes, we may not need to include both perceptual matching task and implicit attitude task.

  3. Using a senior as the character in the treatment video can be considered in future research
    The observational learning video is a video in which a young adult interacts with the robot. Using an older adult as the character might have a different impact on our senior participants and bring us different results.

  4. The plunge of the IAT D1 score in the 2nd round need further investigation
    The meaning behind the plunge of the IAT D1 score in the 2nd round requires further research to investigate, especially since the plunge is more obviously observed in the older adult participants. We are yet not sure if it is practice effects or a decline in implicit attitudes.
